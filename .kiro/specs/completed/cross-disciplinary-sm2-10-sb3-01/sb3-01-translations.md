## Question 1: Energy Transfer Efficiency

### English
**Prompt:** Kannenfeldpark ecosystem: Primary productivity = 8,500 kcal/m²/year. Herbivores consume mean = 850 kcal/m²/year. Carnivores consume mean = 85 kcal/m²/year. Calculate energy transfer efficiency from producers to herbivores (%).

**Hint 1:** Efficiency = (Energy consumed / Energy available) × 100%

**Hint 2:** Producer → Herbivore: (850 / 8500) × 100%

**Hint 3:** Compare result to theoretical 10% rule

**Explanation:** Efficiency = (850 / 8500) × 100% = 10.0%. This matches the theoretical 10% rule, indicating Kannenfeldpark has a healthy, typical temperate ecosystem. Energy loss occurs through respiration, heat, and incomplete consumption. The herbivore → carnivore efficiency (85/850 = 10%) also follows the 10% rule, demonstrating consistent energy transfer across trophic levels.

## Question 2: Shannon Diversity Index

### English
**Prompt:** Rhine River macroinvertebrates (n=200): Mayfly 80 (p=0.40), Caddisfly 60 (p=0.30), Stonefly 40 (p=0.20), Dragonfly 20 (p=0.10). Calculate Shannon index H' = -Σ(pᵢ × ln(pᵢ)).

**Hint 1:** Calculate each term: pᵢ × ln(pᵢ) for all four species

**Hint 2:** ln(0.40) = -0.916, ln(0.30) = -1.204, ln(0.20) = -1.609, ln(0.10) = -2.303

**Hint 3:** Sum all terms and multiply by -1

**Explanation:** H' = -[0.40×(-0.916) + 0.30×(-1.204) + 0.20×(-1.609) + 0.10×(-2.303)] = -[-0.366 - 0.361 - 0.322 - 0.230] = 1.28. This moderate diversity indicates good water quality in the Rhine. Higher H' values (>2.0) indicate greater diversity, while lower values suggest pollution or habitat degradation.

## Question 3: Population Growth Rate

### English
**Prompt:** Basel blackbird population: 2020 = 450, 2024 = 520 (4 years). Using exponential growth Nₜ = N₀ × e^(rt), calculate annual growth rate r.

**Hint 1:** Set up equation: 520 = 450 × e^(4r)

**Hint 2:** Divide both sides by 450, then take natural logarithm

**Hint 3:** Solve for r: ln(520/450) = 4r

**Explanation:** 520/450 = 1.156. ln(1.156) = 0.145. 0.145 = 4r, so r = 0.036 or 3.6% per year. This positive growth rate indicates Basel's urban environment supports healthy blackbird populations, likely due to abundant food sources in parks and gardens, and suitable nesting sites in urban trees.

## Question 4: Trophic Biomass Ratio

### English
**Prompt:** Rhine River biomass (kg/ha): Producers mean = 12,000, Primary consumers mean = 1,200, Secondary consumers mean = 120. Calculate biomass ratio between producers and secondary consumers.

**Hint 1:** Divide producer biomass by secondary consumer biomass

**Hint 2:** 12,000 / 120 = ?

**Hint 3:** Compare to expected 100:1 ratio for two trophic levels

**Explanation:** Ratio = 12,000 / 120 = 100:1. This exactly matches theoretical expectations. Each trophic level retains ~10% of energy, so across two levels (producer → primary → secondary), biomass reduces by 10 × 10 = 100×. This demonstrates the Rhine's food web follows classical ecological principles, with efficient energy transfer supporting a balanced ecosystem.

## Question 5: Carrying Capacity Growth Rate

### English
**Prompt:** Basel urban deer: logistic growth dN/dt = rN(1 - N/K), where r = 0.18/year, K = 200, current N = 150. Calculate current growth rate dN/dt (individuals/year).

**Hint 1:** Substitute values into logistic equation

**Hint 2:** Calculate (1 - N/K) = (1 - 150/200) = 0.25

**Hint 3:** Multiply: 0.18 × 150 × 0.25

**Explanation:** dN/dt = 0.18 × 150 × (1 - 150/200) = 0.18 × 150 × 0.25 = 6.75 ≈ 6.8 individuals/year. As population approaches carrying capacity (150/200 = 75%), growth rate slows. At K, growth stops (dN/dt = 0). This demonstrates density-dependent regulation in Basel's urban deer population, where resource limitation constrains growth.
